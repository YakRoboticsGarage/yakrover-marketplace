# Terminology Research: "Robot" vs Industry Standard Terms

Research date: 2026-03-28

The question: does the construction industry say "robot," or does it use different
terminology? Should a marketplace for autonomous construction equipment call them "robots"?

## What DOTs and Federal Agencies Say

FHWA consistently uses **"UAS" (Unmanned Aerial/Aircraft System)** for drones, not "drone"
and not "robot." Their tech briefs are titled things like "Use of Small UAS for Construction
Quantity Estimation." TxDOT's official program is the "UAS Program" and requires flights to
comply with UAS policies. MDOT research uses "UAV" (Unmanned Aerial Vehicle) interchangeably
with UAS.

For ground-based systems, FHWA uses **"robotic system"** in specific contexts (e.g., "Robotic
System for Condition Assessment of Concrete Bridge Decks") and **"automation"** as the broader
category. NIST uses "A-UGV" (Autonomous Unmanned Ground Vehicle) in technical standards.

Key finding: DOTs and federal agencies **never say "robot" as a standalone category.** They say
UAS, UAV, robotic system, or autonomous equipment. However, FHWA does use "robotics" as a
field name -- a 1995 Public Roads article is titled "Robotics in Highway Construction and
Maintenance," and current research continues to reference "robotics technologies."

AUVSI rebranded from "Unmanned" to **"Uncrewed"** in 2022, now adopted by GAO and NOAA. This
shift hasn't reached DOT specs yet, where "unmanned" still dominates.

## What Construction Companies Say

General contractors and equipment operators use **function-specific terms**, not "robot":
- **"Drone"** for aerial systems (67% of major GCs reported using drones as of 2024)
- **"Autonomous equipment"** or **"autonomous machine"** for self-driving heavy iron
- **"Machine control"** or **"grade control"** for automated dozers/excavators
- **"Robotic total station"** for automated survey instruments

Turner Construction, one of the largest GCs, published a case study titled "Advancing Robotics
in Construction with Spot" -- they used **"robotics"** and **"robot"** without hesitation when
referring to Boston Dynamics Spot. But for drones, they say "drone." For autonomous dozers,
they say "autonomous equipment."

The pattern: **"robot" is reserved for things that look like robots** (legged machines, arms,
humanoid forms). Equipment that looks like normal heavy machinery is called "autonomous
equipment" even if it operates identically.

## What Survey Firms Say

The survey industry has its own well-established vocabulary:
- **"Robotic total station"** (universally accepted; Trimble, Leica, and Topcon all use this)
- **"UAS survey"** or **"drone survey"** for aerial photogrammetry
- **"Autonomous survey"** is emerging but not yet standard

ASPRS (American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing) uses **"UAS"** in their
certification program: "ASPRS Certification of UAS Mapping Professionals." They do not say
"drone" in formal contexts.

Key finding: survey professionals already say **"robotic"** routinely (robotic total station).
The word is not foreign to them. But they would never call a drone a "robot."

## What Equipment Manufacturers Say

**Caterpillar** uses **"Cat Command"** as the brand and **"semi-autonomous"** or **"autonomous"**
as the descriptor. Their 2026 press release: "Cat Autonomy Solutions." They never say "robot."
Their system is an "operator-assisted, semi-autonomous technology." The machinery is still
called an excavator, dozer, or compactor -- just with autonomous capability.

**Komatsu** brands it **"Intelligent Machine Control"** (iMC). The dozer is still a dozer.

**Built Robotics** has "Robotics" in the name but calls their product the **"Exosystem"** -- an
"autonomy kit" that retrofits existing equipment. They invented the role **"Robot Equipment
Operator" (REO)** but their marketing centers on "autonomous construction equipment." Their
autonomous pile driver is the "RPD 35" -- "Robotic Pile Driver."

**Boston Dynamics** calls Spot a **"robot"** outright: "agile mobile robot." Their construction
page emphasizes "robot" and "mobile robot." They lean into the word.

**Skydio** says **"autonomous drone"** -- never "robot." Their webinar: "Autonomous Drones
Ready for Work."

**DJI Enterprise** uses **"drone"** in consumer/commercial contexts but **"UAS"** for government.

**Gecko Robotics** says **"robot"** -- their wall-climbing inspection devices are unambiguously
robots (TOKA, TOKA Flex). Their clients in oil/gas and defense accept the term.

## What Industry Publications Say

**ENR (Engineering News-Record)** uses both "robot" and "autonomous equipment" depending on the
machine type. A drilling machine is a "drilling robot." A self-driving haul truck is
"autonomous equipment."

**CONEXPO-CON/AGG 2026** -- the industry's largest trade show -- used **"robots" and
"autonomous equipment" interchangeably** in their coverage. Categories included "autonomous
machines," "robotic systems," and simply "robots." Honda showed an "Autonomous Work Vehicle."
Advanced Construction Robotics showed "IronBOT."

**Construction Dive** headline: "Built Robotics releases 1st fully autonomous construction
machinery." Note: "autonomous construction machinery," not "robot."

**For Construction Pros** (2026 CONEXPO recap): "autonomous equipment" was one of the 5 key
trends alongside electrification and AI.

## The Word "Robot" -- Perception Analysis

**Where "robot" works in construction:**
- Legged machines (Spot) -- universally called "robot"
- Task-specific arms and manipulators (rebar-tying robot, drilling robot, bricklaying robot)
- Wall-climbing inspection devices (Gecko's TOKA)
- Layout/marking machines (Civ Robotics' "construction layout robot")
- The compound "robotic total station" is industry-standard

**Where "robot" sounds wrong to a superintendent:**
- An autonomous Cat D6 dozer -- that's "autonomous equipment," not a "robot"
- A DJI Matrice doing a site survey -- that's a "drone," not a "robot"
- A Komatsu excavator with iMC -- that's "machine control," not a "robot"

**The dividing line:** if it replaces a piece of traditional equipment, it's "autonomous
[equipment type]." If it's a new form factor that didn't exist before, it's a "robot."

**Does "robot" sound too sci-fi?** Not anymore. CONEXPO 2026 used it freely. Turner
Construction uses it. Built Robotics raised $64M+ with it in the name. The industry has moved
past the sci-fi stigma for actual robotic form factors. But applying "robot" to what is clearly
a dozer or a drone will confuse people.

## Recommendation: Should We Change "Robot"?

**Keep "robot" but don't use it as the only term.**

The marketplace should use a **tiered vocabulary** that matches how the industry actually talks:

1. **"Robot"** -- use for: legged platforms (Spot), task-specific manipulators (rebar bots,
   drilling bots), layout robots, inspection crawlers. These ARE robots and the industry calls
   them robots.

2. **"Drone" / "UAS"** -- use for: aerial systems. Say "drone" in the UI (that's what 67% of
   GCs call them) and "UAS" in specs and government-facing contexts.

3. **"Autonomous equipment"** -- use for: self-driving dozers, excavators, haul trucks,
   compactors. These are equipment with autonomy, not "robots."

4. **"Robotic survey"** -- use for: robotic total stations, autonomous layout systems. The
   survey industry already uses "robotic" here.

**For the marketplace name itself:** "Robot Marketplace" is fine as an umbrella brand. It's
memorable, clear to outsiders, and the industry has accepted "robotics" as the field name even
when individual machines aren't called "robots." Built Robotics, Gecko Robotics, Advanced
Construction Robotics, Civ Robotics -- the successful companies use "robotics" in their names.

**The risk of changing:** calling it "Autonomous Equipment Marketplace" is accurate but generic
and forgettable. "Robot" has energy. The construction industry is warming to it. Keep it.

**One change worth making:** in listing categories and filters, use the industry's own terms
(drone, autonomous dozer, robotic total station) rather than forcing everything under "robot."
Let the brand say "robot" while the taxonomy speaks the buyer's language.
